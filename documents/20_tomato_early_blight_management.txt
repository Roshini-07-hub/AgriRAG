TOMATO EARLY BLIGHT MANAGEMENT
================================

1. INTRODUCTION
Early blight of tomato is caused by Alternaria solani. It is one of the most common and destructive foliar diseases of tomato, causing yield losses of 20–50% under favorable conditions.

2. SYMPTOMS
- Dark brown to black circular spots with concentric rings (target board pattern)
- Yellow halo around lesions
- Lesions first appear on older lower leaves
- Severe defoliation in advanced stages
- Stem lesions: Dark sunken cankers at soil level (collar rot)
- Fruit lesions: Dark sunken spots at stem end

3. FAVORABLE CONDITIONS
- Temperature: 24–29°C optimal
- High humidity (>90%) and prolonged leaf wetness
- Alternating wet and dry periods
- Nutritional stress (nitrogen deficiency)
- Wounds from insects or mechanical damage

4. DISEASE CYCLE
- Fungus survives in infected plant debris and soil
- Conidia spread by wind, rain splash, and insects
- Infection occurs through stomata and wounds
- Disease progresses from lower to upper leaves

5. MANAGEMENT STRATEGIES

5.1 Cultural Control
- Use disease-free certified seeds
- Crop rotation (avoid solanaceous crops for 2–3 years)
- Remove and destroy infected plant debris
- Avoid overhead irrigation; use drip irrigation
- Maintain proper plant spacing for air circulation
- Stake plants to keep foliage off ground

5.2 Chemical Control
- Seed treatment: Thiram 3 g/kg or Carbendazim 2 g/kg
- Foliar spray at first symptom appearance:
  * Mancozeb 75 WP at 2.5 g/L
  * Chlorothalonil 75 WP at 2 g/L
  * Iprodione 50 WP at 1.5 g/L
  * Azoxystrobin 23 SC at 1 mL/L
- Spray at 7–10 day intervals
- Alternate fungicides to prevent resistance

5.3 Biological Control
- Trichoderma viride 1% WP at 4 g/L
- Pseudomonas fluorescens 0.5% WP at 5 g/L

6. RESISTANT VARIETIES
- Arka Rakshak (hybrid, tolerant)
- Pusa Hybrid-4
- Avoid highly susceptible varieties in disease-prone areas

7. ECONOMIC THRESHOLD
- Spray when 5% leaf area is affected
- Preventive sprays during humid weather
